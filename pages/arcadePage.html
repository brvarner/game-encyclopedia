Arcade Games

Skip to Video

Arcade games, while still prevalent, once ruled the world in profitability and innovation. Modern games like Breath of The Wild and Expedition 33 don't exist without the cultural impact of games as simple as Pong and Donkey Kong paving the way.

Nolan Bushnell, who later founded both Atari and Chuck E. Cheese, worked with a team to bring a mainframe game called Spacewar! to the masses. Though he was an engineering student in the 60s, he'd also spent summers working at an amusement park with coin-operated pinball and electromechanical games. He realized that if he could combine the coin-operated mechanism with this new form of entertainment, he'd make history.

After creating custom hardware and finding publishing partners, Bushnell and his team released Computer Space in 1970. Though this game didn't light the world on fire, it was the beginning of Atari whose first game, Pong, kickstarted the arcade revolution with simplified controls and an addictive gameplay loop.

On this foundation built by Atari, legendary companies like Sega, Nintendo, and Namco sprang to life. Nintendo's mascot, Mario, started life as Jumpman in a game where he tried to rescue Princess Peach from Donkey Kong. Years later, he's the face of gaming for even casual video game fans.

As these companies vied for our quarters, they spent more and more on research and development that led to better hardware, which meant better graphics and sound. Eventually, the war moved to our homes with home consoles coming into popularity. Even though arcade games are now more of a nostalgic item, their place in gaming history cannot be denied.
